üë• Main Characters

George Milton

Role: Lennie's caretaker and protector.

Traits: Smart, protective, frustrated, responsible, cynical yet dreams of better life.

Transformation: From hopeful dreamer to tragic realist who must destroy his own dream to save Lennie from a worse fate.

Lennie Small

Role: George's companion; mentally disabled.

Traits: Physically strong, childlike, innocent, loves soft things, forgetful, dependent.

Symbolism: Represents innocence destroyed by harsh reality and the impossible American Dream.

Candy

Role: Old ranch hand with one hand.

Significance: Fears uselessness; loses his old dog (foreshadows Lennie); invests in George and Lennie's dream.

Represents: The fate of the aged and disabled in society.

Curley's Wife

Role: Only woman on the ranch (nameless).

Traits: Lonely, attention-seeking, unfulfilled dreams of stardom.

Significance: Victim of sexism; her death triggers the tragedy and represents broken dreams and isolation.

Crooks

Role: Black stable hand, isolated by racism.

Traits: Intelligent, bitter, lonely, proud, cynical.

Represents: Racial segregation and discrimination; desperately lonely yet protective of himself.

Slim

Role: Respected mule driver; moral center.

Traits: Wise, understanding, respected, compassionate.

Significance: The only one who fully understands George's final act; represents ideal masculinity and moral authority.

üé≠ Major Themes

The American Dream

The unattainable dream of land ownership and independence. George and Lennie's dream of owning a farm represents hope but ultimately proves impossible. Steinbeck suggests the American Dream is an illusion for working-class people.

Loneliness & Isolation

Every character is profoundly lonely: George and Lennie seek companionship in each other; Crooks is isolated by racism; Curley's wife by sexism; Candy by age and disability. The ranch represents a world where human connection is nearly impossible.

Power & Powerlessness

Explores who has power (white men like Curley) versus the powerless (women, minorities, the disabled, the poor). The social hierarchy creates victims and perpetuates suffering.

Friendship & Loyalty

George and Lennie's relationship is unique on the ranch. Their bond represents the human need for companionship but also the burden of responsibility. George's final act is both ultimate loyalty and tragic betrayal.

The Nature of Strength & Weakness

Lennie is physically strong but mentally weak; George is physically small but intelligent. True strength comes from compassion and understanding (Slim), not physical power or cruelty.

üìç Key Locations

The Riverbank (Salinas River)

Symbolism: Eden/Paradise ‚Äì natural, peaceful, represents freedom and the dream.

Significance: Opening and closing location; paradise before and after the fall.

The Bunkhouse

Symbolism: Harsh reality of working life; temporary, impermanent.

Significance: Where dreams are discussed but reality dominates; represents the transient life of ranch workers.

The Barn

Symbolism: Death and destruction.

Significance: Where Lennie kills the puppy and Curley's wife; the place where dreams die.

Crooks' Room

Symbolism: Segregation and exclusion.

Significance: Physical manifestation of racism; Crooks' only space but also his prison.

üåü Key Symbols

The Dream Farm

Represents the American Dream, freedom, safety, and independence. Its impossibility symbolizes the false promises of American society.

Lennie's Mice / Soft Things

Innocence and gentleness that inadvertently destroys. Lennie's love of soft things leads to death and foreshadows the tragic ending.

Candy's Dog

Represents the fate of the old, weak, and useless. Its shooting foreshadows Lennie's death ‚Äì a mercy killing to prevent suffering.

The Rabbits

Lennie's fixation; represents innocence, his simple dreams, and childlike nature. Also represents the unattainable future.

Lennie's Dead Puppy

Foreshadows Curley's wife's death. Shows Lennie's inability to control his strength and the pattern of innocent destruction.

Curley's Glove

Full of vaseline; represents Curley's insecurity about his masculinity and his possessive, crude relationship with his wife.

‚úçÔ∏è Literary Devices & Elements

Foreshadowing

The death of the mouse, the killing of Candy's dog, and the death of the puppy all foreshadow Lennie's death. The incident in Weed (touching the girl's dress) foreshadows the tragedy with Curley's wife.

Circular Structure

The novel begins and ends at the riverbank, creating a circular narrative that emphasizes the inevitability of the tragedy and the cyclical nature of the workers' lives.

Naturalism

Steinbeck uses a naturalistic style ‚Äì characters are victims of social and economic forces beyond their control. Environment and heredity determine fate.

Biblical Allusions

Garden of Eden imagery (riverbank), Cain and Abel parallels (George and Lennie), and the fall from innocence. The title itself references Robert Burns' poem about destroyed dreams.

Animal Imagery

Lennie is repeatedly compared to animals (bear, horse, dog) ‚Äì emphasizes his natural innocence but also his place in the predatory social hierarchy.

Dramatic Structure

Written like a play with distinct scenes, limited settings, and heavy dialogue. Each chapter is almost a separate act building to the climax.
