üìñ Plot Summary

A respected London lawyer, Mr. Utterson, investigates strange occurrences involving his friend Dr. Jekyll and the mysterious, violent Mr. Hyde. Through a series of unsettling events, Utterson discovers that Jekyll has created a potion that transforms him into Hyde, allowing him to indulge his darker impulses without consequence. However, Jekyll gradually loses control over the transformations, becoming trapped as Hyde. The novella ends with Jekyll's confession and death, revealing the terrifying consequences of trying to separate good and evil within oneself.

üé≠ Major Themes

Duality of Human Nature

The central theme: every person contains both good and evil. Jekyll's experiment attempts to separate these elements, but this proves impossible and destructive.

Repression & Victorian Society

The pressure to maintain respectability leads to hidden vices. Jekyll creates Hyde to escape social constraints and indulge forbidden desires.

Science vs. Religion/Morality

Jekyll's scientific ambition leads him to transgress moral boundaries. The novella questions whether scientific progress should have ethical limits.

The Danger of Secrecy

Secrets and concealment enable Hyde's violence. The characters' reluctance to gossip and pry allows evil to flourish unchecked.

Loss of Control

Jekyll gradually loses the ability to control his transformations, symbolizing how indulging evil impulses can lead to complete moral collapse.

Reputation & Public Image

Victorian gentlemen are obsessed with maintaining respectability. This facade of propriety hides dark secrets and moral corruption.

‚úçÔ∏è Literary Devices & Elements

Gothic Elements

Dark, mysterious atmosphere; supernatural transformation; violence; psychological horror; Victorian setting with gloomy streets and fog

Foreshadowing

Hyde trampling the child predicts his later murder; Jekyll's will hints at his connection to Hyde; Lanyon's horror foreshadows the revelation

Narrative Perspective

Third-person limited (through Utterson) creates mystery; shifts to first-person in Lanyon's letter and Jekyll's confession for revelation and intimacy

Suspense & Mystery Structure

Information is deliberately withheld; the reader discovers truth alongside Utterson, creating tension and engagement

Metaphor

Hyde as metaphor for repressed desires; the transformation as metaphor for moral deterioration; London streets as labyrinth of Victorian morality

Imagery

Vivid descriptions of Hyde's repulsive appearance; dark, foggy London streets; the contrasting doors of Jekyll's house

Allusion

Biblical references (Cain, Satan); classical mythology; contemporary scientific ideas about evolution and degeneracy

Juxtaposition

Jekyll (civilized, respected) vs. Hyde (savage, despised); respectable streets vs. sinister back alleys; public propriety vs. private vice

üë• Key Characters

Dr. Henry Jekyll

Role: Protagonist and tragic figure. Respected doctor who creates Hyde to separate his good and evil natures. Represents the dangers of repression and the impossibility of dividing human nature.

Edward Hyde

Role: Jekyll's evil alter ego. Described as deformed, repulsive, and primitive. Represents unleashed id, Victorian fears of degeneration, and the monster within civilized man.

Mr. Gabriel John Utterson

Role: Lawyer and narrator for most of the story. Rational, loyal friend who investigates the mystery. Represents Victorian respectability and the voice of reason.

Dr. Hastie Lanyon

Role: Jekyll's former friend and colleague. Witnesses Jekyll's transformation and dies from the shock. Represents conventional science and the limits of rationality.

Mr. Poole

Role: Jekyll's loyal butler. His loyalty and concern for Jekyll drive the final confrontation. Represents faithful service and the observant working class.

Scientific Progress: Darwin's evolution theory challenged religious views. The story reflects anxieties about science transgressing moral boundaries and the fear of degeneration.

üí° Key Takeaways

  • Human nature cannot be divided into pure good and evil; attempting to do so leads to destruction
  • Repressing one's darker impulses rather than acknowledging them can lead to dangerous consequences
  • Scientific ambition without ethical constraints can create monsters
  • Victorian society's obsession with reputation created conditions for hypocrisy and hidden vice
  • Evil, once unleashed and indulged, becomes increasingly difficult to control
  • The novella serves as a psychological study of the divided self and moral responsibility
